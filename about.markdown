In one of his books, I don't remember which, Tim Ferris describes himself as a "professional dilettante." The term stuck with me strongly, and I have since used it liberally, especially in the case of answering the commonly-asked question, "What do you do?" In fact, I am now introducing myself as a professional dilettante.

The anthropological value of language, the calculus of semiotics, the etymology of words, the grammar of machines and automata, the axioms and incompletenesses of formal systems: a dilettante is easily distinguished by the breadth of her knowledge. Words are so powerful that it is very easy to co-opt knowledge with understanding.

She becomes a professional dilettante when the surface-level is perturbed by seeds of minima, giving rise (or descent) to depth.

"Chasing theta" is a term. The theta referred to by this term (the greek letter θ can symbolize a wide variety of mathematical constants) represents the statistical parameter used in writing the likelihood function, L(θ\|x) = f(x\|θ), used in [Bayesian probabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_interpretations).
